# AAS Marketing ROI Report

## Executive Summary

AAS operates as a commitment contract platform with a 0.5% platform fee and 100% forfeiture on failed commitments. The unit economics are unusual: the platform **loses money on every successful user** and profits exclusively from failures. This inverted incentive structure, combined with the product's niche positioning and irreverent tone, creates a marketing profile where organic/viral channels dramatically outperform paid acquisition.

---

## Unit Economics

### Revenue Model (per $100 commitment)

| Scenario | Platform Receives | Refunds | Stripe Fee (non-refundable) | Net to Platform |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| User succeeds (refund) | $96.80 | -$99.50 | -$3.20 | **-$2.70** |
| User fails (forfeiture) | $96.80 | $0 | -$3.20 | **+$96.80** |

The 0.5% platform fee ($0.50) does not cover Stripe's ~3.2% processing fee on refunds. The business model is entirely dependent on forfeiture revenue.

### Expected Revenue Per Commitment

Using stickK's published data as a baseline — commitment contracts with financial stakes show ~78% success rates in self-reported studies (Royer et al., 2015; stickK internal data). Adjusting downward for selection bias and honor-system gaming:

| Assumed Success Rate | Revenue per $100 Commitment |
|---|---|
| 50% | $47.05 |
| 60% | $36.50 |
| 70% | $27.15 |
| 80% | $16.66 |

**Working estimate: $27-37 per $100 commitment** (60-70% success rate).

At the average commitment size of ~$107 (stickK's implied average from $69M / 644K commitments), expected revenue is **$29-40 per user commitment**.

### Lifetime Value

If the average user makes 2.5 commitments over their lifetime (estimated from stickK's 644K commitments across an estimated 200-300K unique users):

| Success Rate | LTV per User |
|---|---|
| 60% | $91.25 |
| 70% | $67.88 |

**Working LTV estimate: $68-91.**

## Market Sizing

### Total Addressable Market

- US self-improvement market: $13.2B (2022), 5.5% CAGR (Grand View Research)
- Global behavioral health apps: $3.8B (2023), 15.5% CAGR (Mordor Intelligence)
- Commitment contracts / accountability tools: no reliable market size data; likely <$100M

### Serviceable Market

Primary cohort: tech workers and knowledge workers (25-40) with disposable income and self-improvement intent.

- ~8M US tech workers (BLS)
- ~15% have used a goal-tracking or accountability app (estimated from app download data)
- ~5% would consider staking money on a goal (extrapolated from stickK's 644K lifetime users over 16 years vs US population)
- Serviceable addressable market: ~60,000-100,000 potential users in the US

### Competitive Landscape

| Platform | Model | Users/Commitments | Revenue Est. |
|---|---|---|---|
| stickK | Free (donations to charity on failure) | 644K commitments, $69M staked | Unknown; likely <$5M/yr |
| Beeminder | Subscription + penalties | ~10K active users (est.) | ~$1M ARR (reported) |
| AAS | 0.5% fee + forfeiture | 0 (launch) | $0 |

The market is small but underserved. stickK has had minimal product innovation since 2008. Beeminder targets quantified-self enthusiasts. Neither has strong brand identity or viral mechanics.

- Of those, 15-25% make a first commitment: 75-500 commitments
- Revenue: $2,000-$18,500 in month 1

The product's name (AAS/ass), concept, and irreverent tone are inherently shareable. The "is it dumb if it works?" framing is engineered for social media debate. **Organic virality is the most realistic growth vector.**

**Paid acquisition is not viable at current economics.** Even the most optimistic CAC ($83) exceeds revenue per commitment at high success rates. Paid channels only become breakeven if:
- Average commitment size exceeds $300, OR
- Success rate drops below 40% (ethically problematic to optimize for), OR
- Users make 4+ commitments each (unproven)

## Key Risks

1. **Regulatory**: Commitment contracts are legal (stickK precedent), but state-level money transmission laws could apply. No states have challenged stickK in 16 years, but AAS should consult a lawyer before scaling.

2. **Stripe fee erosion**: The platform loses $2.70 per successful $100 commitment. If success rates exceed 85%, the business becomes unprofitable even with forfeitures. This is somewhat self-correcting (higher success rates mean the product works, driving more users).

3. **Honor system abuse**: Users can trivially claim 100% achievement and get refunded. The opaque review process adds friction but isn't a real barrier. If word spreads that verification is performative, forfeiture rates (and thus revenue) drop to near zero.

4. **Market size ceiling**: The intersection of "has disposable income," "wants accountability," and "would use this specific website" may be smaller than projected. stickK accumulated 644K commitments over 16 years — AAS shouldn't expect to match that in year 1.

5. **Single-channel dependency**: If organic/viral growth doesn't materialize, the economics don't support pivoting to paid acquisition.

---

## Recommendations

1. **Do not invest in paid marketing.** The unit economics don't support it. Spend $0 on ads.

2. **Maximize launch buzz.** Product Hunt, Hacker News, Twitter, and relevant subreddits are the highest-ROI activities. The product's name and concept are the marketing.

3. **Invest in content/SEO.** Write about behavioral economics, commitment contracts, and the psychology of accountability. The product is its own case study.

4. **Consider raising the platform fee to 2-3%.** At 0.5%, the platform loses money on successes. At 3%, a successful $100 commitment nets $0.80 instead of -$2.70. Users paying $3 instead of $0.50 is unlikely to affect conversion. Alternatively, charge the fee separately from the Stripe transaction.

5. **Track the success rate obsessively.** It is the single most important business metric. Revenue is a direct function of failure rate. If the product works too well, it becomes unprofitable.

6. **Build referral mechanics.** "I just bet $100 I'd finish my side project" is a naturally shareable statement. Add share-to-Twitter on commitment creation. Consider referral incentives (reduced fee for referrer).

---

## Bottom Line

AAS has strong unit economics on the back of forfeiture revenue, but the addressable market is niche and paid marketing is uneconomical. The realistic path is organic growth powered by the product's inherent virality. A reasonable 12-month revenue target is **$17,000-100,000** depending on launch execution, with near-zero marketing spend. The most important investment is not in marketing — it's in making the product weird and shareable enough that people talk about it for free.

The 0.5% fee is a branding choice, not a revenue driver. Revenue comes from failure. The more people fail, the more money the platform makes. The more the product helps people succeed, the less money it makes. This tension is the core strategic question AAS needs to resolve as it scales.
